The Hudjo way

Omotenashi.
Selfless hospitality.

In Japan, there's a concept called Omotenashi — anticipating what someone needs before they ask. It's what the best cafés, shops and local businesses do every single day.

When you host with Hudjo, you bring that same care to the cyclist outside. You take their bike, keep it safe, hand it back when they return. And in doing so, you earn income, drive footfall, and become part of the infrastructure that makes your neighbourhood thrive.

Why we exist

Cycling is the most human way to move through a city. But without somewhere safe to leave your bike, people don't ride. We fix that — by turning the spaces that already exist into the infrastructure that cycling needs.

What we're building

The founder story

It started with a bike that wasn't there.

David Gamble was eight years old when his bike was stolen. He'd leaned it against a railing, gone inside, and come back to nothing. That small, sharp loss — the confusion, the injustice of it — never quite left him.

"I remember thinking: why isn't there somewhere safe to leave it? Why does no one care about this?"

Thirty years later, David still cycles everywhere. And he still sees the same problem at every destination — bikes locked to signposts, chained to railings, left and hoped for. The infrastructure for cycling has improved enormously. The parking hasn't moved at all.

Hudjo was built to close that gap. Not with lockers or technology for its own sake, but with something simpler and more human: trust. A local business that cares. A host who takes your bike and hands it back. The peace of mind that turns a maybe-I'll-cycle into a yes-I-will.

The name comes from Hojo — Japanese for security and prosperity. The philosophy comes from Omotenashi. And the mission is the same one that's been in David's head since he was eight years old, standing on an empty pavement, looking for a bike that wasn't there.
